# Electrician Common Core Level 3

Level 3 represents advanced apprenticeship training, focusing on **three-phase power systems, complex motors, transformers, and generating systems**. This is the final Common Core level before specialization—after completing Level 3, you'll choose between Construction Electrician or Industrial Electrician pathways for Level 4.

You'll combine **10 weeks (300 hours) of intensive technical training** with approximately **1,500-2,000 additional work-based hours**, bringing you to around 4,500 cumulative hours—three-quarters of the way to Red Seal certification.

## Before You Begin

**Work Experience Requirement**:
You'll need approximately **4,500 cumulative hours total** before Level 3 technical training (typically 1,500-2,000 additional hours after Level 2), representing approximately three years into your apprenticeship.

**Prerequisites**:
- Successfully completed Level 2 technical training
- Passed the Level 2 standardized exam (minimum 70%)
- Logged required work-based hours
- Employer sign-off confirming intermediate competency
- Good standing with SkilledTradesBC

## Technical Training Curriculum

Level 3 focuses on three-phase power systems and advanced electrical equipment:

### Three-Phase AC Circuits and Electronic Circuits (34% of curriculum)
- Three-phase AC theory and calculations
- Delta and wye configurations
- Three-phase power calculations
- Balanced and unbalanced loads
- Electronic circuit fundamentals
- Solid-state devices and applications

### Advanced Measuring and Testing Equipment (2%)
- Advanced multimeter functions
- Oscilloscopes and waveform analysis
- Megohmeters for insulation testing
- Clamp-on ammeters and power analyzers
- Thermal imaging cameras
- Specialized test equipment

### Three-Phase Plan Interpretation (4%)
- Complex three-phase distribution drawings
- Load schedules and panel schedules
- Single-line diagrams for industrial systems
- Riser diagrams for multi-floor buildings
- Coordination with other trades

### Three-Phase Services and Metering (4%)
- Three-phase service entrance equipment
- Service sizing for commercial and industrial loads
- Three-phase metering installations
- Demand metering and load management
- Power factor monitoring

### Three-Phase Distribution Equipment (2%)
- Switchgear and switchboards
- Panelboards for three-phase systems
- Distribution transformers
- Voltage regulation equipment
- Power distribution units (PDUs)

### Three-Phase Grounding and Ground Fault Detection (2%)
- Three-phase system grounding methods
- Ground fault detection systems
- Ground fault protection devices
- System grounding vs. equipment grounding
- Separately derived system grounding

### AC and DC Generating Systems (10%)
- Generator operating principles
- Prime movers (diesel, natural gas, turbine)
- Standby and emergency power systems
- Automatic transfer switches (ATS)
- Paralleling switchgear
- Generator sizing and load calculations
- Battery systems and UPS fundamentals

### Three-Phase Transformers and High-Voltage Transformers (10%)
- Three-phase transformer connections (delta-delta, delta-wye, wye-wye, wye-delta)
- Transformer paralleling
- High-voltage transformer applications
- Pad-mounted transformers
- Dry-type vs. liquid-filled transformers
- Transformer testing and maintenance

### Advanced Wiring Devices (2%)
- Specialty receptacles and plugs
- Locking devices and twist-lock configurations
- High-amperage devices
- Explosion-proof wiring devices
- Hazardous location equipment

### Three-Phase Motor Starters (9%)
- Three-phase motor starter configurations
- Reversing starters
- Reduced voltage starting methods
- Wye-delta starters
- Part-winding starters
- Motor control center (MCC) installations

### Variable Frequency Drives (VFDs) (4%)
- VFD operating principles
- VFD installation and wiring
- Programming and commissioning
- Harmonic mitigation
- Applications for motor speed control
- Energy savings with VFDs

### Comprehensive Motor Coverage - AC and DC Motors (17%)
- **Single-phase motors**: Split-phase, capacitor-start, capacitor-run, shaded-pole
- **Three-phase motors**: Squirrel-cage induction, wound-rotor, synchronous motors
- **DC motors**: Series, shunt, compound motors
- Motor nameplate data interpretation
- Motor selection for applications
- Motor protection and overload sizing
- Motor testing and troubleshooting procedures
- Bearing maintenance and lubrication
- Alignment and installation best practices

- **Minimum 70%** on in-school assessments
- **Minimum 70%** on the standardized Level 3 exam
- Satisfactory completion of all practical assignments

## Financial Support During Training

**Employment Insurance (EI) Benefits**:
- Up to **$668 per week** for 10 weeks
- Apply 4 weeks before through [WorkBC Apprentice Services](https://workbcapprenticeservices.ca)

**Canada Apprentice Loan**:
- Up to **$4,000 per period** (interest-free)
- Maximum $20,000 total

**BC Training Tax Credit** (NEW at Level 3!):
- **$2,000 refundable tax credit** upon Level 3 completion
- Claim on your tax return using Form T1014
- Enhanced credit available for First Nations individuals and persons with disabilities (additional $500)

## Work-Based Training After Level 3

**Progression to Level 4**:
You'll need **6,000 cumulative hours total** before Red Seal certification (approximately 1,500 additional hours after Level 3), representing four years of full-time employment.

**Wage Progression**:
Typically 60-80% of journeyperson wage at this level, with higher rates reflecting your advanced skills.

## Specialization Decision Point

**Between Level 3 and Level 4, you must choose your specialization** in consultation with your employer:

### Construction Electrician (309A)
**Work Environments**:
- Residential, commercial, and institutional buildings
- Transportation systems and marine installations
- Entertainment venues
- New construction and renovation projects

**Career Characteristics**:
- Project-based work with variety
- Often seasonal employment patterns
- Working for electrical contractors
- Opportunities for entrepreneurship (own contracting business)

### Industrial Electrician (442A)
**Work Environments**:
- Manufacturing plants and production facilities
- Mines, smelters, and pulp mills
- Shipyards and oil/gas operations
- Hydroelectric dams and industrial facilities

**Career Characteristics**:
- Full-time plant positions with steady year-round employment
- Shift work opportunities with premium pay
- Maintenance, troubleshooting, and repair focus
- Deep specialization in industrial controls

**Can You Switch Later?**
Yes, with cross-credit. Certificate holders in one stream receive credit for Levels 1-3 and 4,500 work hours, and must complete only Level 4 of the other stream.

## What Comes Next

Upon completing Level 3, you'll:

1. **Choose Your Specialization**: Construction or Industrial pathway for Level 4
2. **Complete Level 4 Training**: Specialized 10-week program in your chosen stream
3. **Finish Work Hours**: Reach 6,000 cumulative hours
4. **Write Red Seal Exam**: Achieve interprovincial certification
